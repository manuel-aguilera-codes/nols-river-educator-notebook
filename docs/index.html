📖 Introduction — How to Use the REN

The River Educator Notebook (REN) does not teach you to be a guide: it assumes you already know how to live, travel, and teach on rivers. It provides the educational goals and curricular content that NOLS has reached common agreement on, along with proven teaching techniques.

Core idea: Activities define the course; instruction supports the activities. The river usually teaches more than the instructor.

River Area Learning Objectives

  • Demonstrate effective strokes
  • Maneuver the craft in moving water
  • Read water: features, hazards, and lines
  • Lead and participate in group travel
  • Execute basic rescues
  • Tie knots and manage ropes for rescue and rigging

Use the REN alongside the WEN (Wilderness Educator Notebook) and LEN (Leadership Educator Notebook).

Ch. 1 — Intentional Planning for Learning

A well-planned 60-min lesson usually distills to ~25 digestible minutes. Two formats:

Extended Content Lesson (20–60 min)

Progression: Sell It → Show It → Do It → Review It

  • Sell It (1–5 min): Motivate and prepare. Name the skill, give context, explain why it matters short- and long-term. Use a joke, game, or story.
  • Show It (5–15 min): Demonstrate one skill at a time. Whole–Part–Whole: show complete → break it down → show complete again. Go half-speed, pause at transitions, exaggerate rotation.
  • Do It (10–15 min): The heart of the lesson. Students apply learning to real scenarios, games, or challenges.
  • Review It (5 min+): Strategic questions to digest and store in long-term memory.

Strategic Questions

  • Lower-order: knowledge and comprehension
  • Higher-order: analysis, synthesis, application — aim to balance both
SWBAT: "Students Will Be Able To…" — formula for writing measurable learning objectives.

Focused Skill Lesson

Shorter, for an isolated technique. Starts from a Basic Task and branches out:

  • Simplifying task: isolates one component for those who struggle
  • Extending task: increases difficulty for those who already have it
  • Applying task: takes the skill into real application for fast learners

S-O-S Model (Space–Observe–Space)

  • Space: let them explore new information; be comfortable with pause and silence
  • Observe: watch without talking, identify simple coaching points, evaluate the right moment to intervene
  • Space: let them practice with structure and a deadline; follow up at the right moment
Effective feedback: descriptive (what they did well) + prescriptive (how to improve). Avoid the generic "good job."
Ch. 2 — Intentional Observations

Everyone is responsible for observing each other's lessons with objective (not subjective) findings — this makes feedback easier to deliver.

4 Systematic Peer-Observation Forms

1. Lesson Clarity and Demo Effectiveness

  • Does the instructor teach at an appropriate pace? Give examples? Allow enough practice time?
  • Verify comprehension? Review key points in the closure?
  • Demonstrator: precise? Ensures everyone can see? Breaks down a limited number of elements in sequence?

2. Time Management — 3 Components

  • Managerial episodes: organizing, transitions, equipment → minimize
  • Cognitive activity: readings, visuals, demonstrations
  • Motor activity: hands-on tasks → maximize

3. Coaching and Verbal Behaviors

  • General: avoid it ("good job" without detail)
  • Descriptive: specific about what was done well
  • Prescriptive: what to try on the next attempt

Goal: all coaching is descriptive + prescriptive simultaneously.

4. Questions — Taxonomy

Lower-order (Knowledge, Comprehension) vs. Higher-order (Application, Analysis, Synthesis, Evaluation). Wait at least 3 seconds after each question.

Build an open coaching culture: many instructors associate burnout with stopping learning. Have students fill out these forms too — it reinforces their growth as educators.

"Judgment Comes from Experience"

Incidents occur when objective hazards overlap with subjective ones. Judgment develops through experience + reflection. Don't sweep conflicts under the rug — addressing them creates a safer, more productive environment.

Ch. 3 — Leadership on the River

NOLS Leadership Model 4-7-1

  • 4 Roles: Designated Leader, Active Follower, Peer Leader, Self-Leader
  • 7 Skills: Expedition Behavior, Competence, Communication, Judgment & Decision-Making, Tolerance for Adversity & Uncertainty, Self-Awareness, Vision & Action
  • 1 Imprint: Leadership Style / Conflict Style

Designated Leadership Roles

  • Gear Managers: campsite selection, groover, hand wash, water, gear organization, Roosters (wake-up)
  • I-Team of the Day: two students join the i-team; observe meetings, paddle lead and sweep
  • Master Educators: teach a 15–30 min class from any notebook
  • Lead Technicians: re-teach river rescue or a boating skill
  • Lead Medics: handle simple medical issues, lead first-aid scenarios, carry the first-aid kit

Lead Boat — Key Coaching Points for DLs

  • Recognize the point of no return (last eddy before committing to a rapid)
  • Stand up to get a better view when approaching
  • Can you see the bottom? Is there an eddy for the whole group? Is there an obvious line? → If "no" to any: land scout
  • Manage subjective hazards (don't only focus on distance)
Finger signal system:
1 finger = "give us space, we'll work it out"
2 fingers = "coaching, we want suggestions"
3 fingers = "help! we need the I-team NOW"

F.A.C.E.T.S. — Error Heuristics

Familiarity · Acceptance · Commitment · Aura of Expertise · Sensitive to Time · Social Proof

Control vs. Chaos

Behaviors that increase control: competence, communication, organization, well-being, culture.

Behaviors that increase chaos: unpracticed skills, poor instruction, disorganization, fatigue/hunger/thirst, laziness and complacency.

Traveling LITE

  • Lessons Learned — learn something new every day
  • Intentions Set — "I can" attitude; write intentions down
  • Thanks Given — daily gratitude
  • Embrace Mystery — humility; without mystery there is nothing to learn

Three frames of focus: Place (root yourself, practice good stewardship), Community ("find your tribe and you will thrive"), Self (deep connection leads to a more aware self).

Ch. 4 — Living on the River

Camp Selection

Consider: flash floods, widowmakers, lightning, wildfires, bear protocols, LNT, land-management agency protocols, groover placement, hand-wash station, garbage management.

Water Disinfection Matrix

  • Heat: bacteria ✔ viruses ✔ protozoa ✔ (a rolling boil is sufficient)
  • Filtration (≤1 µm): protozoa ✔ bacteria difficult viruses ✗
  • Filtration (≤0.2 µm): bacteria ✔
  • Halogens (iodine/chlorine): protozoa ✗ bacteria ✔ viruses ✔
  • Chlorine dioxide: Cryptosporidium "maybe" — requires ≥4 h contact time
  • UV light: works, requires clear water

Hand Washing

Vigorous scrubbing ≥20 s. Water with 5 drops of chlorine sterilizes the water — not your hands. One in four illnesses reported at NOLS involves communicable disease transmission.

Catholes

200 feet from the river; verify local regulations.

Dutch Ovens

Briquette ratio: 3:1 (top:bottom). ~2 briquettes per inch of diameter. Rotate the oven ⅓ every 10 min and the lid ⅓ in the opposite direction. Remove bottom heat at ⅔ of the cooking time.

The Groover

Name origin: early rocket boxes left grooves on your backside. Separate bucket for urination. Bio-hazard box + paper box.

Ch. 5 — River Travel

River Safety Talk — PPE

  • PFDs, helmets, closed-toe footwear
  • Whistle, saw, knife, carabiners, pulleys, prusik, throw bag
  • NEVER dive headfirst into a river (death, paralysis, brain trauma)
  • Defensive swimming (on back, feet downstream) vs. aggressive swimming (face down, crawl stroke)

River Signals

  • STOP: horizontal bar with arms or paddle
  • All Clear / Go: one arm or paddle straight up
  • Direction: lower the "all clear" 45° toward the preferred side (point to the positive, not the hazard)
  • Eddy out: one hand rotating over head, the other pointing to the eddy
  • Help / Emergency: three long whistle blasts while waving paddle/helmet/PFD
  • Are you OK?: hand on head = yes
  • Whistle: three long blasts = emergency; one blast = attention

Always repeat and pass back any signal received.

Scouting with WORMS

  • Water — where does most water flow? Downstream V?
  • Obstacles — rocks, holes, strainers, undercuts — which are critical to avoid?
  • Route — route options; which is most direct?
  • Markers — visual references identified from shore to use at water level
  • Swimming — where is a swimmer most likely to have trouble? Where would rescue be most effective?
Decision: use the probability/consequence continuum. If the probability of making the line is high and the consequence is low, running the rapid is reasonable.

Hydrology — 3 Difficulty Factors

  • Gradient (feet per mile) — misleading on its own
  • Volume (CFS/CMS)
  • Hazards — strainers, undercuts, sieves, foot entrapment, remote environment

Strainers

Fixed: stationary objects (rooted trees, fences) — most eventually become dynamic.

Dynamic: the most dangerous — not polished or de-branched, can span 100+ feet with 30-foot branches, or float just below the surface.

Self-rescue near a strainer: 1) Swim aggressively away. 2) Aggressively climb up and over it. 3) "Stall and side-step."

Holes: "Smiling" vs. "Frowning"

  • Smiling (open shoulders): easier to escape
  • Frowning (closed shoulders): harder to escape, more dangerous

Lead and Sweep Boats

Lead: experienced paddler who identifies hazards and eddies out when needed. Sweep: closes the group, carries basic rescue gear and first-aid kit, capable of executing rescues.

Ch. 6 — River Rescue

Rescue Priorities

  1. Rescuer safety
  2. Group safety
  3. Patient rescue
  4. Victim recovery
  5. Equipment recovery

Rescue Zones

  • 🟢 Green: beginners, clients, spectators, EMS — not actively participating
  • 🟡 Yellow: 10–30 feet from the water; full river PPE, rescue education and training
  • 🔴 Red: the river itself; full PPE, ≥16–24 h rescue training, capable of self-rescuing by aggressive swimming

Rescue Options — Lowest to Highest Risk

  1. Signal — shout with hand signal (lowest risk)
  2. Reach — extend a stick/paddle from shore
  3. Wade — rescuer enters the water
  4. Throw — throw rope; introduces entrapment potential
  5. Boat — can be less risky than rope if executed well
  6. Swim — rescue swimmer; one of the most dangerous options
Rope entrapment: NEVER wrap rescue rope around hands or body. Do not tie yourself to a rope unless using a type-V PFD with a specialized harness.

Entrapment Rescues

  • Heads-up (breathing) → stabilize
  • Heads-down (not breathing) → extract immediately

Anchors — GREAT

  • Great — trees >6 inches diameter, living and well-rooted; rocks you can't wrap your arms around
  • Redundant — minimum two protection points
  • Equalized — each point receives the same force
  • Angle — do not exceed 90°; at 120° each piece receives 100% of the load
  • Timely — build them before you need them; practice often

Mechanical Advantage

Force units = # of haulers × mechanical advantage. An adult pulls ~50% of their body weight.

Order to try: 1:1 with redirect → 1:1 with vector → 3:1 → 5:1 → 9:1 compound. Always with a redirect pulley. Systems >12:1 increase risk of equipment failure. Mastering the "3-5-9" + 4:1 pig rig is a reasonable expectation.
The redirect pulley is CRITICAL: must change the direction of pull ~90°. No one should stand inside the angle formed by the system.

Swimming — Key Rules

  • NEVER dive headfirst into a river
  • Swiftwater entries: crouch with staggered feet, explode outward hitting the water flat, PFD first, head hyperextended
  • Defensive swim: on back, feet downstream
  • Aggressive swim: face down, crawl stroke, head up, flutter kick
  • Ferry angle: 45–60° loses less ground; 90° arrives faster but drifts farther downstream

Key Knots

Figure eight on a bight, double bowline with backup, butterfly knot, munter hitch, clove hitch, prusik, auto-block, flat overhand ("euro death knot" — needs tail 4–6× the knot length).

Terminology: Knot (in the rope itself), Hitch (attached to an object), Bend (joins two ends), Loop, Bight, standing end, running/working end, tail (4–6 inches).

Ch. 7 — Canoeing

Stroke Concepts

  • Biomechanics: strong, comfortable body positions; torso rotation provides position, not necessarily power
  • Stroke mechanics: how the blade moves the canoe
  • Vertical blade close to the canoe → forward momentum; diagonal extension (sweeps, prys, draws) → corrections and turns

MAT Framework (Canoe in Moving Water)

  • Momentum — cross the grain when approaching the eddy
  • Angle — eddy lines are typically crossed at ~80°, not the commonly cited 45°; open angle = quick short turn, closed = slow wide turn
  • Tilt — lean into the turn, set the tilt early, hold it longer than expected

Forward Stroke — CATCH–POWER–RELEASE

  • Catch: wind up torso, blade enters at the toes, control hand at eye level
  • Power: unwind the torso; the stroke lasts the length of the unwind
  • Release: as the blade passes the knee, drop the control hand to the lap, thumb out

Physics — Bow Wake and Carving vs. Spinning

The bow wake acts as a bumper holding the bow while the stern floats free. A tilted canoe carves an arc (like a snowboard on edge). A flat canoe "spins out" (stern skids).

Lining and Portaging

  • Portage: carry the boat overland. Plan loads, route, bear awareness.
  • Lining: control the descent from shore with painters (1.5–2 boat lengths). Never wrap painters around your hands.
  • Tracking: traveling upstream using the boat's angle against the current.
  • Hauling: moving the boat by walking in the water.

Canoe Self-Rescue — 3 Steps

  1. Paddle — grab it!
  2. Position — defensive, upstream of the canoe, push it aggressively downstream
  3. Partner — check your partner, communicate the plan
Tandem communication: the best pairs use little or no verbal communication in the middle of a rapid. Non-verbal communication develops by practicing in both ends of the boat.
Ch. 8 — Whitewater Rafting

Raft Types

  • Oar rafts: 12–24 ft; supports solo oarsman or carries gear for kayakers
  • Paddle rafts: 9–16 ft, 2–9 people; mobility, precision, teamwork

Guide Commands

Speak with conviction. Minimize syllables. Use directional commands ("back right" implies the left side paddles forward). "Get down" lowers the center of gravity better than "hold on."

Crew Forward Stroke (CPR)

  • Catch: lean forward, arms extended, shaft vertical
  • Power: sit up using the core, pull to the hip ("pull the raft to the planted blade")
  • Release

Flip Drills

Daily activity. Consider depth, swimming ability, run-out. Advanced drill: swim → grab perimeter line → hug thwart with legs → land in the raft as it flips back over.

Oar Raft — Strokes

  • Push: push forward; allows reading the river, not as powerful as the pull
  • Pull: the most powerful stroke; uses quads and hamstrings; always mindful of the downstream oar

Lining Up for Big Hits

Aim directly at the obstacle with good momentum. If it becomes a side surf: ship the upstream oar, move passengers to the downstream side, put the downstream oar into green water.

Ch. 9 — Whitewater Kayaking

ACA Progression

Equipment → Stroke Concepts → Wet Exits → Roll → Flatwater → Moving Water → Strategies & Risk Management → Advanced (surfing, squirts, enders)

APE Framework (Eddy Turns and Ferries)

  • Angle — between 45 and 85° depending on destination and currents
  • Power — momentum to cross the eddy line in control
  • Edge — tilt toward the turn with a powerful vertical stroke on the inside
Edge timing: don't tilt until the new current hits the bow. The edge prevents the stern from skidding and stops the upstream edge from catching.

Wet Exit — T-T-P-P

  • Tuck — protect torso and head until nearly touching the deck
  • Tap — slap the sides of the kayak (foundation for bow/T-rescue)
  • Pull — slide hands along the coaming, grab the skirt handle, pull it forward
  • Push — hands on deck behind hips, push the boat; release legs before swimming

Kayak Stroke Concepts

  • Paddler's Box: imagine a beach ball between the blade and your chest; keep hands in front of the body
  • Active blade: keep one blade in the water at all times
  • Leans: aggressive (whitewater default, slight forward tilt), neutral (flatwater), defensive (leaning back = poor execution)
  • Positive targeting: focus on the bottom eddy, not the scary wave

Kayak Forward Stroke

  • Catch: blade enters at the toes, shaft ~45° or more vertical
  • Power: unwind from the shoulders, twist with abdominals to the hip
  • Release: at the hip, cock the wrist, exit cleanly

Roll — Sweep Roll (7-Step Progression)

  1. Body synergy (cooperative movement)
  2. Boat rotation — "birds-knees-fish"
  3. Set-up position (~20 reps)
  4. Finish position (~20 reps)
  5. Reverse finish-to-setup
  6. "Gradually Going Deeper Drill"
  7. Gradually remove instructor support (taps if student needs help)
Boat rotation mantra: "birds-knees-fish" — look to the sky for birds, hook the knee, look to the bottom for fish.

Recommended Paddle Length

  • 5'4"–5'8" → 192–196 cm
  • 5'8"–6'1" → 194–200 cm
  • >6'1" → 198–204 cm
Ch. 10 — Packrafting
The central danger of packrafts: "it's too easy." Their stability builds confidence quickly in Class III with a fraction of the time and skill it would take in a kayak or canoe. Boat control ≠ hazard recognition.

Brief History

Modern packrafting was born in Alaska in adventure races and Brooks Range traverses. NOLS' first stand-alone packraft course was in Alaska in 2011. NOLS routinely runs Class III with students who have only a few days of experience.

Packraft Self-Rescue

Flipping the boat

Keep paddle and boat → go to the end with the backpack → cross arms, grab the perimeter line → unflip it by uncrossing your arms.

Re-entry

Go to one side → hold paddle perpendicular over the tube → other hand grabs thigh strap/seat → float feet to the surface → push with arms while kicking → keep center of gravity low, rolling the body → into paddling position.

Rigging Modes

  • Rig to carry: for multiple portages or short/calm sections; pack lashed to bow
  • Whitewater rig: weight even and low; two dry bags under the deck; more weight toward stern and low
  • Backpack mode: everything inside; deflate the packraft before packing

Group Management Considerations

  • Small pods with one instructor each
  • A "probe boat" on sections with wood, unknown channels, braided rivers, or blind corners
  • Swiftwater exit for shore without eddies: ferry upstream to slow down, release thigh straps, exit quickly

Repair

  • Pinholes: Tyvek/Tenacious tape on inflated, dry boat
  • Tears: patch + Aquaseal
  • Leaking valve: glue the base and wrap with dental floss

Minimalist Mindset

Toothbrush ceremony: commit to cutting toothbrushes in half. Traveling light is a mindset, not just a technique.

Target pack weight: 30–40% of body weight. Full PPE (PFD + helmet + paddle + packraft + skirt + thigh straps): 17–25 lb total.

💡 Key Takeaways for the Expedition
Activities → Instruction Activities define the course; instruction supports them. Don't take academic content too seriously or too lightly.
Plan with intention Sell It → Show It → Do It → Review It. Differentiate basic / simplifying / extending / applying tasks to serve the full range of the group.
Effective feedback Descriptive + prescriptive, always. Balance higher- and lower-order questions. Avoid generic "good job."
Objective + subjective hazards Most incidents occur where they overlap. Avoid complacency (F.A.C.E.T.S.).
Delegate leadership creatively Lead boat, I-team of the day, finger signals — while the I-team always retains final authority over risk.
Scout → Decide → Execute WORMS to scout. Probability/consequence to decide. MAT (canoe/raft) or APE (kayak) to execute.
Rescue: principles over absolutes KISS. Prioritize rescuer safety. Always use a redirect pulley. Start simple before escalating to mechanical advantage.
NEVER dive headfirst Swiftwater entries only in controlled, supervised environments. Flat entry, PFD first, head hyperextended.
The packraft deceives Boat control ≠ hazard recognition. Ease generates overconfidence.
Traveling LITE Learn something every day, set intentions, give thanks, embrace mystery — for yourself and by modeling it for your students.
